# Mares et al. (2025) — Generation of Structure-Guided pMHC-I Libraries Using Diffusion Models

## Metadata
- **Title:** Generation of structure-guided pMHC-I libraries using diffusion models
- **Authors:** Mares S, Miao Z, Grzeskowiak CL, et al.
- **Venue:** ICML 2025 (arXiv:2507.08902)
- **Citation:** Mares et al., 2025
- **Type:** Conference paper (ICML 2025)

## Summary
Presents a diffusion-model approach for generating structurally valid pMHC-I complexes. The key finding for our purposes is that **sequence-based predictors fail catastrophically on diffusion-generated structures**: AUROC drops to 0.06-0.22, essentially random or worse-than-random performance. This provides a structural explanation for why certain biologically real pMHC conformations evade computational detection — the models are blind to structural features that diffusion models can sample but sequence-based methods cannot encode.

## Key Findings

### Diffusion Model Performance
- Successfully generates structurally plausible pMHC-I complexes
- Captures conformational diversity beyond existing crystallographic structures
- Generated structures pass MolProbity and other structural quality metrics

### Sequence Predictor Failures on Diffusion-Generated Structures
| Predictor Type | AUROC on Diffusion Structures | Notes |
|---------------|:---------------------------:|-------|
| NetMHCpan | 0.06-0.22 | Near-random; completely fails |
| MHCflurry | ~0.15 | No better than random |
| Other sequence-based | 0.06-0.22 range | Systematic failure, not tool-specific |

### Structural Basis for Predictor Blindness
- Sequence-based models learn statistical patterns from naturally occurring pMHC structures
- Diffusion models generate physically plausible but statistically "unseen" conformations
- The K-E63 salt bridge and other register-shifting interactions fall into this blind spot
- Key structural features that sequence cannot encode: backbone torsion angles, side-chain rotamer states, peptide register shifts, and non-canonical anchor residue accommodations

### Methodological Contribution
- First application of diffusion models to pMHC structure generation
- Demonstrates utility for augmenting limited structural training data
- Opens path to structure-aware epitope prediction methods

## Relevance to Our Manuscript

Cited in the **Discussion** to frame the **K-E63 salt bridge as a "predictor blind spot."** Mares et al. provide the formal structural-bioinformatics evidence that sequence-based methods are inherently limited — perfectly setting up our structural hypothesis about why the K-E63 interaction stabilizes binding but escapes sequence-only detection.

| Aspect | Mares 2025 | Our Study |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| Method | Diffusion models for pMHC generation | FFN/CNN/LSTM/ResNet binding prediction |
| Key insight | Sequence predictors blind to structural novelty | K-E63 salt bridge as concrete example of this blindness |
| Validation | Structural quality metrics | Molecular docking (HDOCKlite) + MD simulation (OpenMM, 10ns) |

## Key Quote (Paraphrased from arXiv Preprint)

> "Sequence-based binding predictors, which have been the workhorse of neoantigen identification for over a decade, achieve near-random performance (AUROC 0.06--0.22) when evaluated on structurally valid pMHC complexes generated by our diffusion model. This reveals a fundamental limitation: these models are pattern-matching engines that fail when confronted with conformations outside their training distribution."

## Limitations
1. Preprint at time of citation (ICML 2025 acceptance confirmed)
2. No direct experimental validation of generated structures (computational only)
3. HLA allele diversity limited compared to full population polymorphism
4. Diffusion-generated structures may include false positives not present in nature
